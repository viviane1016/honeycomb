## Why

The cost of catching a regression grows by level. A defect caught by builder tests costs one cell and one builder. Caught by scribe verify, it costs one builder plus reverting one merged cell on the feature branch. Caught by queen accept, it costs multiple merged cells plus operator re-triage. Caught at human PR review, it costs operator time (the scarcest resource). Caught in production, it is the most expensive of all. The V-model positions each verification at the cheapest level that can plausibly catch the class of defect it targets.

Author-as-verifier keeps the verifier honest because they must compare the implementation against *intent*, not just against text. Independent reviewers read the spec and check if the diff matches. Author-reviewers read the spec and ask: "Does this implement what I meant to ask for?" They spot implementations that satisfy the literal requirement but miss the point—missing edge cases the requirement author had in mind, or taking a technically-correct but semantically-wrong path. The cost of this model is clear: the colony loses a fully-independent second pair of eyes inside the dispatch. That is why human PR review remains the outermost gate, the final decision point before merge. The colony's internal V-model (builder tests, scribe verify, queen accept) is advisory; the human operator is the ultimate arbiter. This is not a substitute for human review—it is a cost-mitigation strategy that catches cheap defects early while preserving human judgment for the hard calls.

Both verify and accept ship in v0.5.0 as advisory gates; see `wing_bees/stage-verify` and `wing_bees/stage-accept` for harness behaviour.
